# The Admirative Mood (Habitore)

<MoodBadge name="admirative" /> is one of the more striking features of Albanian morphology. Most Indo-European languages express surprise, irony, or reported speech through prosody, particles, or auxiliary constructions. Albanian conjugates it.

A speaker says <Example verbId="punoj" mood="admirative" tense="present" person={3} number="singular" /> when they have just discovered, with some emotional charge, that someone works. The neutral indicative <Example verbId="punoj" mood="indicative" tense="present" person={3} number="singular" /> simply states the fact.

## Where it sits in the system

The admirative is one of six finite moods in standard Albanian:

- <MoodBadge name="indicative" /> — neutral statement
- <MoodBadge name="subjunctive" /> — possibility, intent, dependence
- <MoodBadge name="conditional" /> — hypotheticals
- <MoodBadge name="admirative" /> — surprise, irony, evidentiality
- <MoodBadge name="optative" /> — wish, blessing, curse
- <MoodBadge name="imperative" /> — command (2nd person only)

Among these, only the admirative and the optative lack direct equivalents in English. The admirative is sometimes called *evidential* by linguists who emphasize its reported-speech use; *habitore* (literally "amazement") emphasizes its expressive use. Both are correct.

## How it's built

The admirative present is the participle stem (with the participle suffix dropped) plus the admirative endings: `-kam / -ke / -ka / -kemi / -keni / -kan`. These are derived from `kam` ("to have") fused onto the participle root — a contracted compound that has grammaticalized over the centuries.

For <VerbLink lemma="punoj" />, participle `punuar` → drop `-r` → `punua` → add `-kam` → <Example verbId="punoj" mood="admirative" tense="present" person={1} number="singular" />.

## Where you'll see it

In conversation, the admirative tags the speaker's astonishment or doubt. In journalism and folklore, it marks reported speech where the speaker is not asserting the truth of the statement — equivalent to English's "supposedly" or "allegedly," but baked into the verb's morphology.
